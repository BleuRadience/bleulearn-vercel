DOCUMENTED LESSON PLAN — Grades K-12

Introducing Fractions Through the Comparison Bar Model

Documented Learning Objective

By the end of this lesson, scholars will be able to draw an accurate comparison bar model for any fraction comparison problem and solve using the four-step protocol.

Free Resources (No Textbook)

Thinking Blocks (thinkingblocks.com, free); Math Learning Center fraction strips (mathlearningcenter.org/apps, free)

Vocabulary (Frayer Model Pre-Teach)

  • numerator: the top number — how many parts we have
  • denominator: the bottom number — how many equal parts the whole is split into
  • comparison: side-by-side measurement to find a difference or ratio

Instruction — Steps

  1. Concrete: physical fraction strips. Show 3/4 vs 2/4. Which is larger? Show the difference physically.
  2. Pictorial: draw the comparison bar on the board — top bar 3/4, bottom bar 2/4, difference bracket on the right.
  3. Financial Architecture connection: draw the documented racial wealth gap as a comparison bar ($171,000 vs $17,000) and calculate the ratio.
  4. Abstract: scholars solve three problems, bar model always before the equation.

Discourse

Turn-and-Talk (Grade 4) / Partner Discussion (Grade 5): A classmate says you don't need the bar model if you already know the answer. Document your response.

BleuLearn Pause

"Draw a comparison bar model of two fractions you choose. In one sentence, document why you drew the bar before the equation."

Exit Ticket

"I show you 5/8 and 3/8. Draw the comparison bar model, label all parts, write the equation, and solve."

Differentiation

  • Bridge: physical fraction strips for the full practice period; like denominators only.
  • Grade-Level: unlike denominators; find common denominator using bar reasoning.
  • Gifted: apply the comparison bar model to the racial wealth gap — what fraction of white median wealth would three pooled Black families represent?

Archive Assignment

Access the Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances (federalreserve.gov) and build a comparison bar model using the 50th vs. 90th wealth percentile for a group of your choice.
