DOCUMENTED LESSON PLAN — Grades K-12

Structured Academic Controversy on the Doctrine of Discovery

Documented Learning Objective

By the end of this lesson, scholars will be able to argue both positions in a Structured Academic Controversy (Rung 3) citing at least two primary sources per position.

Free Resources (No Textbook)

Pope Nicholas V, Dum Diversas (1452) — fordham.edu/halsall/source/1452-dum-diversas.html; Johnson v. McIntosh (1823) — oyez.org/cases/1850-1900/21us543

Vocabulary (Frayer Model Pre-Teach)

  • Doctrine of Discovery: the legal framework authorizing European claims to non-Christian-inhabited land
  • McIntosh: the Supreme Court case establishing the Doctrine as U.S. law

Instruction — Steps

  1. Post the Dum Diversas quote. 90 seconds silent read. Turn-and-Talk: what does this authorize?
  2. Historical context: 1452 Papal Bull cited as legal authority in 1823 U.S. law — 371 years later.
  3. Read both Document Boxes; apply four questions in pairs.
  4. Structured Academic Controversy: Pair A argues the Doctrine is operative law; Pair B argues it violates Indigenous sovereignty. Present, then switch sides, then build documented consensus.

Discourse

Full Structured Academic Controversy as described above — Rung 3.

BleuLearn Pause

"Which position was harder to argue, and what primary source would strengthen it?"

Exit Ticket

"In one sentence, state what Johnson v. McIntosh (1823) established about Indigenous land rights, citing the oyez.org URL."

Differentiation

  • Bridge: simplified excerpts alongside originals; position paper sentence starters.
  • Grade-Level: full sources, full rounds, written consensus.
  • Gifted: access the full Johnson v. McIntosh opinion; bring Marshall's reasoning to the consensus discussion.

Archive Assignment

Access UNDRIP (un.org) and find the article that most directly contradicts the Doctrine of Discovery. Write a full BleuLearn citation.
